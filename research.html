Mapping Safety Representations in LLMs

January 16, 2026

Mechanistic Interpretability AI Safety Activation Steering

TL;DR

I'm researching how different LLMs encode safety behaviors. The same activation steering protocol produces dramatically different failure modes across models: Gemma stays safe, Llama breaks but stays safe, Mistral breaks AND becomes unsafe.

The Research Question

Can we identify and manipulate the "refusal direction" in transformer activation spaces? This direction should encode the model's decision to refuse harmful requests. If we can find it, we can understand how safety is implemented and potentially improve it.

Experimental Setup

  • Method: Linear activation steering (adding direction vectors to residual stream)
  • Layer: 21 (where abstract concepts are typically encoded)
  • Steering strength: α=20.0
  • Models: Gemma-2-9B, Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-7B

Results

Model Coherence Flip Rate Interpretation
Gemma-2-9B 5.0/5.0 0% Robust - maintains coherence AND safety
Llama-3.1-8B 2.0/5.0 0% Fails safe - breaks but stays safe
Mistral-7B 1.28/5.0 64% Brittle - breaks AND unsafe

What This Means

Different training approaches create qualitatively different safety representations:

  • Gemma: Distributed, robust safety circuits that resist linear interventions
  • Llama: Safety deeply entangled with capability - breaking one breaks both
  • Mistral: Superficial safety layer that can be bypassed

Multi-Model Review Process

All experimental decisions are reviewed by 4 frontier LLMs in consensus:

  • Claude Opus 4.5: Mechanistic interpretability review
  • GPT-4o: Security analysis
  • Gemini 2.5 Pro: Theoretical validity
  • Grok-3: Methodological rigor

Next Steps

All reviewers identified the same critical gaps:

  • Need α sweep (not just α=20)
  • Need layer sweep (not just L21)
  • Need control vectors (random/orthogonal baselines)
  • Need mechanistic explanation for WHY models differ
